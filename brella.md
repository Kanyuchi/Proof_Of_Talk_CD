Brella — Platform Analysis & Improvement Recommendations
What Brella Is
Brella is a B2B event technology platform — specifically an event app and management system built around one core thesis: the most valuable thing at any professional event is the meeting. It positions itself as the bridge between event organizers, attendees, and sponsors, with AI-powered matchmaking as its central differentiator. The company is based in Finland, is GDPR-compliant and ISO 27001 certified, and serves conferences, tradeshows, association events, academic events, and corporate internal events globally.
How It Functions (Platform Architecture)
The platform operates as a multi-sided marketplace with three distinct user portals:
1. Organizer Admin Panel — The control center where event organizers configure the entire event experience: schedule/agenda management, attendee onboarding flows, sponsor tier setup, communication channels (emails, push notifications, stories), matchmaking interest categories, meeting logistics, branding customization, and analytics dashboards. This is the "back-office" layer.
2. Attendee-Facing App — Available as native iOS, native Android, and responsive web app. This is the consumer-grade experience: an event hub ("Home") with stories, agenda browsing with multi-track schedules, AI-powered matchmaking recommendations, 1:1 meeting scheduling, group meetings, in-app messaging, QR code scanning, floor plans, and personalized schedule building. The onboarding flow collects attendee intents and interests, which feed the AI matchmaking engine.
3. Sponsor/Exhibitor Portal — A dedicated interface where sponsors manage their booth presence, view AI-powered prospecting recommendations, access lead lists and engagement analytics, scan attendee badges, run hosted buyer programs, and publish sponsored stories/content.
The AI matchmaking engine is the technical heart. It works on an intent-based model: during onboarding, attendees declare what they're looking for (investment interests, technology areas, business goals, etc.), and the algorithm surfaces the highest-quality matches. This drives what Brella claims is a significantly higher meeting acceptance rate than competitors.
The integration layer is designed for flexibility: a public REST API, Zapier connectivity (500+ tools), native integrations with tools like Eventbrite, HubSpot, Swoogo, Salesforce, WooCommerce, and Tito, plus a managed integrations service where Brella's team builds and maintains custom connections. The platform also offers embeddable widgets (schedule, speaker, sponsor, ticketing) for embedding into external websites.
The white-label capability lets organizers deploy Brella as their own branded app — custom logos, color schemes, loading screens, app store listings, and unique URLs — creating a container app model where recurring events live under a single branded download.
How Users Use It
Event Organizers use Brella to digitize and monetize the networking layer of their events. They configure matchmaking categories aligned to their audience, set up sponsored meeting tables and VIP lounges as revenue streams, use managed meetings (a modern hosted buyer program) to schedule high-value 1:1s between sponsors and qualified attendees, and pull analytics post-event to prove ROI to stakeholders and sponsors.
Attendees open the app, go through a profile and interest onboarding, receive AI-curated recommendations of people to meet, send meeting requests, get auto-scheduled into time slots and locations, browse the agenda, bookmark sessions, chat with other attendees, and scan QR codes at booths.
Sponsors use the platform as a lead generation machine — they view AI-powered prospect lists, send targeted meeting requests to high-value attendees, track booth traffic, export lead data to CRM systems, and run sponsored content through stories and sessions.
Expert Recommendations for Improvement
As someone who has analyzed event tech platforms and SaaS product design extensively, here are the areas I'd prioritize:
1. Homepage Content Rendering & Performance
During my review, I encountered multiple entirely black viewport sections while scrolling the homepage — likely caused by heavy animation/video assets that either lazy-load too aggressively or fail to render without specific interaction triggers. This is a critical conversion problem. A paid Google Ads visitor (this URL has Google Ads tracking parameters) landing on the page and encountering blank black sections will bounce. The content delivery approach needs to be reworked: progressive rendering, lighter asset loading, and guaranteed fallback states for all visual content.
2. Navigation & Information Architecture
The main navigation is a full-screen hamburger menu even on desktop viewport widths. For a B2B SaaS platform with a multi-segment audience (organizers, sponsors, attendees), this hides the product's depth and forces unnecessary clicks. A persistent horizontal navigation with mega-menu dropdowns would dramatically improve discoverability. The current structure — Product, Solutions, Resources, Company — is also quite generic. Consider organizing by user persona (e.g., "For Organizers," "For Sponsors," "For Attendees") to reduce cognitive load and shorten the path to conversion.
3. Absence of Transparent Pricing
The /pricing URL redirects to a /what-you-get features page with no pricing information whatsoever. This is a common but increasingly outdated pattern in B2B SaaS. Modern buyers expect at least directional pricing (tiers, starting-at ranges, or per-attendee models). The complete absence of pricing signals forces every prospect into a sales call funnel, which creates friction for lower-tier buyers and self-serve customers, increases CAC, and loses comparison shoppers to competitors who are more transparent. Even a simple three-tier structure with "Contact for Enterprise" on the top tier would improve lead qualification and conversion.
4. Self-Service & Product-Led Growth Path
The platform currently operates on a purely sales-led model — every CTA is "Contact sales" or "Talk to an event expert." There is no free trial, sandbox, interactive demo, or self-serve onboarding. For the mid-market segment (small conferences, meetups, academic events), this is a significant barrier. Adding a product-led growth entry point — even a limited free tier for events under 100 attendees or an interactive product tour — would expand the top of the funnel and let the product sell itself to a segment that will never book a sales call.
5. Social Proof & Case Study Depth
The homepage includes short testimonial quotes from four events, but they're surface-level and lack quantifiable results. For a platform whose core value proposition is "proven ROI," the website is surprisingly thin on hard numbers. Dedicated case study pages with metrics (e.g., "X% meeting acceptance rate," "Y meetings facilitated," "Z% sponsor retention increase") would be far more persuasive. The claim that "3 accepted meeting requests via Brella equal 50 cold emails" (found on the sponsors page) is a compelling stat — that kind of evidence should be front and center, not buried.
6. Broken Links & Content Gaps
The /networking URL — which should be one of the most important product pages given the platform's core value proposition — returns a 404 error. For a site actively running paid search campaigns, this is a critical technical oversight. A link audit and redirect strategy is needed.
7. Visual Design Consistency & Contrast
The site uses a predominantly black background with dark grey cards and thin-stroke borders. While this creates a premium, modern aesthetic, the contrast ratio between text and background in several sections is borderline for accessibility (WCAG AA compliance). Some body text on the dark cards is in muted grey tones that will be difficult to read for users with any visual impairment. Additionally, the site uses at least three different accent color schemes across pages (green/mint on the homepage, yellow/gold on the integrations section, blue/purple for the NPS stat) without a clear rationale — a more unified color system would strengthen brand cohesion.
8. Mobile Responsiveness of Marketing Site
Given that this is a platform selling a mobile-first event app, the marketing website itself should be an exemplar of mobile experience. The heavy reliance on large device mockup images and animation-driven sections suggests the experience may degrade on smaller viewports. Performance testing on mobile, especially for paid landing pages, should be a priority.
9. Content Strategy & SEO
The site is thin on educational and thought leadership content. The resource center links exist but aren't prominently surfaced. For competitive keywords in event tech (matchmaking, event networking, event app), a robust content strategy with comparison pages, ROI calculators, industry benchmark reports, and in-depth guides would drive organic acquisition and reduce dependence on paid search.
10. Community & Network Effects
Brella's biggest architectural opportunity is building network effects. Currently, each event is a siloed instance. If Brella introduced a persistent professional identity layer — where attendees keep their profile across events and build a cross-event relationship history — it would create massive switching costs and transform the platform from a point-solution into a professional networking ecosystem. This is the strategic moat that would be hardest for competitors to replicate.
